Amazing Weaves Distinction Awards — Judging Criteria
Six Dimensions of Excellence.
Designed so culture, enterprise, and governance reinforce one another — building coherent ecosystems, not just award-winning textiles:
Cultural Protection
20%
Documentation of outstanding indigenous weaving with strong cultural narratives, authentic attribution, and craft excellence
Craft Excellence & Eco-Innovation
20%
Technical mastery; natural fiber sourcing; regenerative production models promoting ethical commercialization
LGU Policy & Ecosystem
15%
Strengthened weaving ecosystems through LGU enabling policies, GAD-funded programs, and enterprise development
Community Impact
15%
Responsible raw material sourcing; active skills transfer to younger generations through mentorship and training
Market Access & Value Chain
15%
Demonstrated value chain integration; market linkages; fair benefit distribution across the community enterprise
Sustainable Livelihood
15%
Weaving traditions sustained for women & artisans using GAD resources; replication and adoption models in place
Aligned: Magna Carta of Women · MSME Dev Plan 2023–2028 · ASEAN Gender Mainstreaming Strategy · SDG 5 · GEWE

The Value of What You Carry
Your Weaving Tradition is Irreplaceable.

"I do not want our traditional arts and culture to be lost. The traditional arts of indigenous communities in the Philippines are as universal as they are local."

— Mercedes Zobel, Patron of Philippine Arts · Habi: Art and the Order of Nature · Ayala Museum
What Your Weave Encodes
Motifs that speak of rivers, mountains, rice, and the moon. Ritual patterns connecting communities to the sacred. Knowledge passed for centuries. This is not merely cloth — it is civilization.
T'nalak · South Cotabato
Dream-woven by T'boli masters — each pattern a vision from ancestors. Never replicated exactly. A UNESCO-recognized living tradition.
Piña · Aklan
Woven from pineapple fiber — the queen of Philippine textiles. Painstaking, precious, worn by royalty across Asia.
Yakan · Zamboanga & Basilan
Among the most complex weavings in Southeast Asia — intricate geometric masterpieces of the Yakan people.
Inaul · Maguindanao
The royal textile of the Maguindanao — rich geometric patterning in silk, worn at royal ceremonies and celebrations.
Inabel · Ilocos Norte & Sur
Ancient Ilocano geometric weaves — 400 years of Highland trade identity encoded in cotton.
Royal Recognition
HRH The Prince of Wales: "Philippine indigenous textiles reflect universal principles and provide a view into how our noble ancestors lived sustainably." — Clarence House
