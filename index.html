Your Weaving Tradition is Irreplaceable.

"I do not want our traditional arts and culture to be lost. The traditional arts of indigenous communities in the Philippines are as universal as they are local."

— Mercedes Zobel · Habi: Art and the Order of Nature · Ayala Museum
T'nalak · South Cotabato
Dream-woven by T'boli masters — each pattern a vision from ancestors. Never replicated. A UNESCO-recognized living tradition.
Piña · Aklan
Woven from pineapple leaf fiber — the queen of Philippine textiles. Painstakingly precious, worn by royalty across Asia.
Yakan · Zamboanga & Basilan
Among the most complex weavings in Southeast Asia — intricate geometric masterpieces of the Yakan people.
Inaul · Maguindanao
The royal textile of the Maguindanao — rich geometric patterning in silk, worn at royal ceremonies.
Inabel · Ilocos Norte & Sur
Ancient Ilocano geometric weaves — 400 years of Highland trade identity encoded in cotton.

About the Competition

What is Galing Likha?

Galing Likha is a countrywide initiative that transforms weaving from a heritage & cultural craft to a strategic development pathway towards a sustainable economic asset.

It empowers local governments to invest meaningfully in women-led livelihoods through the 5% LGU GAD budget — potentially unlocking PhP 80 billion — in building ecosystems to preserve, nurture, and elevate weaving as a tool for gender equality, inclusive enterprise, and climate-resilient livelihoods.

"The result is a nationally coherent system where culture, enterprise, and governance reinforce one another."

Selection Process

Island Secretariats for Luzon, Visayas, and Mindanao receive and validate all entries. They confirm the 12″×12″ woven swatch is received and correctly labeled, verify all three signed documents (Community Consent, Authenticity Declaration, Benefit-Sharing Statement), and forward qualified entries to the Island-Wide Selection Committee (IWSC). Incomplete or non-compliant entries are not forwarded.

The Island-Wide Selection Committee (IWSC) evaluates all qualified entries using the six judging criteria. The IWSC selects the Top 5 finalists per island group. Finalists make presentations before the IWSC. After presentations, the IWSC selects the Top 2 Island Winners per island group who advance to the National Finals. Bottom 3 finalists receive ₱30,000 each.

The National Selection Committee (NSC) evaluates the 6 national finalists (Top 2 per island group). National finalists must present the weave AND at least one physical Heritage-to-Use design application (clothing, accessories, interior items, etc.), demonstrating responsible translation of the living heritage into contemporary use. The NSC selects 3 National Winners — one per island group — who receive ₱150,000 each and the Amazing Weaves Distinction Award, with showcase at the ASEAN Trade Show, Grand Ballroom, Okada Manila.

Eligibility Rules

✅
Any LGU may join as Lead Proponent with a Community Partner (association / cooperative / cluster / recognized community)
✅
One entry per LGU featuring one flagship weave tradition for the competition cycle
✅
The featured weave must be produced within the LGU's jurisdiction — indigenous weaves prioritized
✅
Swatch must be exactly 12″ × 12″ (1 square foot) and labeled with entry code — couriered to Island Secretariat
❌
Entries using imported finished textiles or non-local production are ineligible
❌
Entries without Community Consent and all signed mandatory documents will not be evaluated
❌
Misrepresentation, unauthorized motif use, forged endorsements, or plagiarism → disqualification at any stage

Ethical Safeguards

Community Consent & Endorsement

The weaving community must give full, informed consent to participate, including awareness of benefit-sharing terms.

Authenticity & Attribution Declaration

All cultural narratives, motifs, and attribution claims must be accurate and not misrepresent the intellectual heritage of other communities.

Benefit-Sharing Statement

A mutually agreed arrangement must be in place ensuring weavers directly and equitably benefit from prizes, recognition, and commercialization.

All three documents must be signed by both the Local Chief Executive and the authorized Community Partner representative.
