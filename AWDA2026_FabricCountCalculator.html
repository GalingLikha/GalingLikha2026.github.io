How this fits AWDA scoring
This calculator prepares your self-declaration for the application form. In AWDA Doc E v4, fiber authenticity is not scored by live judges from the swatch. Instead: (1) you self-declare fiber % in the application, (2) DOST-PTRI independently validates entries near a threshold boundary, and (3) fiber content appears on your product label — scored under sub-criterion c (finishing and labeling). Thread count informs judges assessing pattern complexity under sub-criterion b.
Who this guide is for
LGU GAD coordinators helping their weaving community submit · Cooperative leaders and master weavers preparing the application · DTI Business Development focal persons supporting applicants in the field · DOST-PTRI field validators conducting fiber verification. No mathematics or laboratory background required.
A
What is thread count — and why measure it?
The simple idea
Thread count is just a way of saying how tightly woven a fabric is. You count how many threads fit inside one inch of cloth in two directions — the up-and-down threads (called warp or ends) and the side-to-side threads (called weft or picks). Add the two numbers together and you have the thread count per square inch.

A fine piña cloth may have 200 threads per inch. A coarser abacá mat may have 50. Neither is better — both are correct for their tradition. What matters is that the count is consistent across the swatch and that the level of skill matches what you declare.
Why AWDA cares
AWDA judges assess pattern complexity and technical difficulty (Track B sub-criterion b) partly by how dense and even the weave is. They also assess finishing quality and labeling (sub-criterion c) — which includes whether the fiber content on the product label matches what your application declares. This calculator helps you prepare an accurate self-declaration and supports DOST-PTRI fiber verification.

C
Understand warp and weft first
Blue box = pick glass on swatch. Green arrows = warp direction. Red arrows = weft direction. Count inside the 1-inch square window.
Memory tip — warp vs. weft
WARP = the threads that are warped (stretched tight) on the loom before weaving begins. They run the length of the cloth — up and down on a standing loom.

WEFT = the thread that goes weft and right (left and right). It is the weaver's shuttle thread that crosses back and forth during weaving. It runs across the width of the cloth.

If you hold the swatch up to light and tilt it at an angle, the warp threads usually catch the light more evenly because they are under more tension. The weft threads often show the pattern.
D
How to count — step by step
Step 1 — Choose your first spot
Place the pick glass near the top-left corner of the swatch. Stay at least 1 cm away from the selvedge (the finished border edge) — thread density near the edge can be slightly different from the body of the cloth.
Step 2 — Count the warp threads
Look through the pick glass. Count each individual thread that runs vertically through the 1-inch square window. Count from left to right. If a thread sits exactly on the window's edge, count it as 0.5. Write the number down immediately.
Tip — count in groups of 10
Use a pin to mark your starting thread. Count 10 threads, then make a mark on paper. Count another 10 and mark again. This prevents losing your place in fine fabrics. For piña cloth (very fine), count in groups of 5.
Common mistake — counting gaps instead of threads
In a plain weave, threads and gaps alternate — it is easy to accidentally count both and get a result twice as high as the real count. Only count the threads themselves, not the spaces between them. When in doubt, try counting a small section of 5 threads with your needle pointing at each one as you count.
Step 3 — Rotate and count weft threads
Turn the pick glass 90 degrees so the 1-inch window now measures across the width of the cloth. Count the horizontal (weft/pick) threads through the window. Write this number down. The warp count and weft count are separate numbers — do not add them until the calculator does it.
Step 4 — Repeat at two more spots
Move the pick glass to the center of the swatch. Count warp and weft. Then move to the bottom-right corner. Count warp and weft. You should now have 3 warp readings and 3 weft readings. Enter all six numbers in the calculator — it averages them automatically.
Tip — why three readings?
Thread count can vary slightly across a swatch due to tension differences during weaving. Three readings from different spots give you a more accurate average. A very large difference between readings (e.g. 60 threads in one spot and 40 in another) indicates uneven tension — worth noting in your AWDA submission as a craftsmanship observation.
E
How to measure crimp
Crimp is the extra length a thread has from weaving over and under other threads — like a zigzag path is longer than a straight line. This matters for fiber content verification.
Crimp % = ((12.6 − 12) ÷ 12) × 100 = 5% — a typical result for abacá cloth
Warp crimp — how to measure
1. Measure the swatch from top to bottom (inside selvedge). Write this down as the fabric length — e.g. 12 inches.

2. At the top edge, use a needle to gently loosen one warp thread. Work from the edge inward, teasing it free slowly.

3. Pull the thread free. Do not pull it tight — let it lie naturally.

4. Lay the thread on your ruler without stretching it. Measure its natural length — e.g. 12.6 inches.

5. Enter both numbers in the calculator. Formula: ((12.6 − 12) ÷ 12) × 100 = 5% warp crimp.
Weft crimp — same method, different direction
Measure the swatch width. At one side edge, unravel one weft thread. Measure its straightened length. Enter both in the calculator. The weft crimp % is calculated separately from the warp crimp %.
Tip — piña fiber is very delicate
Piña fibers are extremely fine and brittle — finer than human hair. When unravelling a piña thread, work millimeter by millimeter with the needle tip. Never grab the thread and pull from the middle. If the thread breaks, try again at a different spot along the edge. Work in good lighting.
Tip — what crimp values to expect
Piña cloth: 1–3% (stiff fiber bends less)
Abacá cloth: 3–6%
Cotton and silk weaves: 3–7%
Above 8%: May indicate loose weave structure or measurement error — recheck.

The AWDA / DOST-PTRI standard applies a 5% crimp correction when verifying fiber content near a threshold boundary.
Do not pull the thread taut when measuring
If you stretch the thread before measuring, you will get a longer length than the natural crimp length. This makes the crimp appear smaller than it actually is and can affect fiber % calculations. Lay the thread on the ruler and let it fall naturally — do not smooth it out with your fingers.
F
Writing your fiber declaration for the application form
The fiber % you enter in Part 3 of the AWDA application is a self-declaration. Write it clearly and honestly — DOST-PTRI verifies shortlisted entries.
Examples — how to write your declaration
"100% abacá fiber; natural indigo dye; sourced from Lake Sebu weavers" → Score 5 band

"85% piña fiber, 15% silk; natural dye predominant; synthetic highlight color disclosed" → Score 4 band

"70% cotton, 30% polyester; mixed dye — natural bark for base, synthetic for bright accent" → Score 3 band

"50% abacá, 50% nylon; synthetic dye" → Score 2 band
Tip — use your supplier invoice
The best source for fiber % is your yarn or thread supplier's invoice or fiber composition certificate. If you process your own raw fiber you know exactly what goes in. If you buy pre-dyed yarn, ask the supplier for a written statement of fiber composition. Keep this document — DOST-PTRI may request it during verification.
Be honest — do not overstate natural fiber content
If you are genuinely unsure of your fiber %, write your best honest estimate and label it as an estimate. Overstating natural fiber % and having it contradict DOST-PTRI test results will raise questions about the entry's credibility across the scorecard — not just on fiber. Honesty in documentation is itself a mark of good practice that the AWDA program rewards.
G
Notes for DTI business development focal persons
Bring a pick glass to every weaving community visit during the AWDA application period. Document thread count readings with a photo of the pick glass placed on the swatch — this supports the Secretariat verification process and helps communities build their documentation package.

For entries near a fiber % threshold boundary — e.g. a community declares 78% natural fiber and the next threshold is 80% — flag these early for DOST-PTRI priority validation. Do not wait until after the island judging event to raise the issue.

The AWDA application form now requires a fiber composition field in Part 3 (added after the June 2026 gap analysis). Assist weavers in completing this accurately. A blank or vague entry (e.g. "natural fiber") will slow Secretariat processing and may prevent the entry from reaching IWSC scoring.

Thread count measurements are most useful for entries near a technique threshold — e.g. a community claiming supplementary weft technique. Help the weaver document the thread count and technique description together for a stronger sub-criterion b case.

Important reminder for LGUs and weavers
Thread count is a reference tool — not a competition score input. AWDA judges assess quality within the tradition's own standard. A T'nalak with 80 threads per inch is not better than one with 60 — what matters is flawless execution of the correct technique for the tradition. Use this table to understand your weave's density range, not to set a target number to chase.
